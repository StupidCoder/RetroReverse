Stunt Car Racer (Amiga) — track viewer

drag to rotate · scroll to zoom

Each of the eight league circuits is the game's own track data, decoded purely in Go from the disk image — no emulation. The disk has no filesystem; a table of handles at $1F0A2 locates each track's byte stream, which is a run-length-encoded list of sections that the loader ($5AE46) walks to lay out the circuit. This viewer re-runs that walk (package track) and lays each circuit out on its 16×16 plan grid — each section's cell is read straight from the section parameter the engine itself uses to look sections up ($5FE04/$64304): the parameter's low nibble is the grid X, its high nibble the grid Y. Consecutive sections are always adjacent cells, so the footprints are smooth closed loops — a diamond (Little Ramp), a rectangle (Stepping Stones), the two inner prongs of the drawbridge (Draw Bridge). Each section also has an elevation — the ribbon is an elevated track on support columns, just like the game's pre-race preview, with the ramps, jumps and Roller-Coaster hills. The track is drawn as a hidden-line wireframe: two rails plus rungs, coloured along the lap (blue → red), columns down to the ground, and the green marker at the start/finish. The decode is entirely from the disk in Go (section parse verified coordinate-exact against the original loader on our Go 68000 core). Crucially, the surface is not one height per section: each section carries an exact per-rung rail-height profile — the engine's own vertex builder ($5C0AA) reproduced in Go and verified coordinate-exact against the original on all eight tracks (cmd/geomoracle). Those profiles are the surface: a smooth run of values is a drivable ramp or rolling hill, a sudden jump is a hard edge — the three pairs of steep lips on Big Ramp, the square gaps between the Stepping Stones, the Ski Jump's launch. No heuristic decides step-vs-slope; the data does. The in-plane shape is exact too: each section's plan outline — the (x,z) rail vertices the engine's $5C6C4 reads from the piece (verified by cmd/planoracle) — is similarity-fitted onto its grid cell, so straights stay straight and curve pieces carry their real arc, with the exact rail width. There is no spline and no nominal width: the whole ribbon is the engine's own geometry, lifted by the exact rail heights whose difference is the real camber.

Drive it (WASD). Press Drive to take the wheel: the car's rigid-body physics — the same `$6185C` frame reverse-engineered in Part V, hand-ported to JavaScript and verified exact in your browser against a trace from the Go reimplementation (the HUD reports the check) — runs live. It provides the acceleration, the suspension bounce over the decoded surface, and the roll/pitch; W/S throttle and brake, A/D steer.
